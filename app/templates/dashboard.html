When our two AI assistants disagree on how to categorize a document, you need to decide which classification is correct.

How does this work?

Two AI assistants (GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet) independently read each document's abstract and assign it to topic categories. When they disagree on the main topic, we need a human to make the final decision.

Your job: Read the document abstract below, look at what each AI chose, and decide which classification makes the most sense. You can pick one of the AI's choices, or create your own custom classification if neither is right.

Project Overview

A complete view of all ferrofluid research papers and patents, organized and categorized by topic.

What is this project?

We have about 4,500 documents related to ferrofluids (magnetic fluids) — a mix of published research papers and US patents. The goal is to sort every document into one of 30 topic categories so we can see what areas have been studied, what has been patented, and where the gaps are.

To do this sorting, we used two separate AI assistants that each read the summary of every document and independently decided which topic it belongs to. When both AIs picked the same topic, we accepted that answer automatically. When they disagreed, a human stepped in to make the final call.

What do the numbers mean?

  • Total Documents — The complete number of papers and patents in the system.
  • Papers — Published research articles from academic journals.
  • Patents — US patent filings related to ferrofluids.
  • Agreed — Both AIs picked the same topic. These are automatically accepted.
  • Disagreed — The two AIs picked different topics. A human needs to decide which one is right.
  • Human Reviewed — A human has already looked at the disagreement and made the final decision.
  • Pending — Documents that haven't been categorized yet.
  • Classified — All documents that have been through the process (whether the AIs agreed, disagreed, or a human reviewed them).

Goal 1: Classified Papers

What is this?

Every research paper has been sorted into a ferrofluid topic. This table shows the result — you can see which topic each paper was placed into, when it was published, and whether the two AIs agreed or a human made the final call.

What do the columns mean?

  • Serial — A short ID for each paper (like P1, P2, P3...).
  • Year — When the paper was published.
  • Title — The name of the paper. Hover your mouse over it to see the full title.
  • Primary — The main topic this paper is about. This is the most important classification.
  • Secondary — The second-most relevant topic.
  • Tertiary — The third-most relevant topic. If a paper only covers one subject, all three may be the same number.
  • Status — "agreed" = both AIs picked the same answer. "human_reviewed" = a person decided.

What do the colors mean?

Each topic belongs to a broader category, shown by color: green = Materials & Properties, blue = Computer Modeling, orange = Experiments & Measurements, pink = Real-World Applications, purple = Reviews & Books.

The table is sorted by year (oldest first), then by topic number. Only papers with a final decision appear here — unresolved disagreements are not shown until a human reviews them.

Just like the papers, every US patent related to ferrofluids has been sorted into one of the 30 topics. Patents go back to 1969 and include filings up to the present day.

Goal 3a: Gap Analysis

What is this?

Now that every paper and patent has a topic, we can ask: "Where has research been done but nobody has filed a patent?" and the other way around. These mismatches are called gaps.

For example, if a topic has 50 research papers but zero patents, that's a gap — it means scientists are studying it but no one has tried to turn it into a product or invention yet. That could be an opportunity.

How to read the charts

  • Bar chart (left) — For each topic number, the blue bar shows how many papers there are and the purple bar shows how many patents. If you see a blue bar with no purple bar next to it, that topic has research but no patents.
  • Line chart (right) — Shows how the most popular topics have grown or declined over time, grouped into 5-year periods. This helps you see trends.

What do the labels in the table mean?

  • both — This topic has at least one paper AND at least one patent. Research and industry are both active here.
  • papers only — Research papers exist but no patents. This is an opportunity gap — the science is there but nobody has patented it yet.
  • patents only — Patents exist but no research papers. This is uncommon.
  • empty — No papers or patents in this topic.

Goal 3b: Patent-Paper Links

What is this?

For every patent, we found the 3 research papers that are most closely related to it. This helps answer the question: "Which research might have led to or inspired this patent?"

How did we find the matches?

The system compared the written summaries of every patent against every paper and measured how similar the words and phrases are. The more words two documents share (especially uncommon, specific words), the higher the similarity score.

What do the columns mean?

  • Patent / Paper — The ID and title of the patent on the left, and the matching paper on the right.
  • Similarity — How closely related the two documents are, shown as a percentage. Higher is better. Above 20% means they share meaningful overlap. Above 40% means they are very closely related.

Each patent shows up to 3 times — once for each of its top matching papers, with the best match first.

Goal 4: Assignee Cross-Reference

What is this?

This section looks for people who appear in both worlds — someone who is listed as an inventor on a patent AND as an author on a research paper, working on the same ferrofluid topic. These people are interesting because they bridge the gap between academic research and real-world inventions.

How does it work?

The system takes all the inventor names from patents and all the author names from papers, then looks for matching names. A match only counts if the person worked on the same topic in both their patent and their paper.

Why are there no matches?

The patent files and paper files store names in very different ways. For example, a patent might list "Smith J" while a paper might list "John Smith" or "J. Smith". Because the names are formatted so differently, the system can't tell they're the same person. To fix this, the names would need to be cleaned up by hand or matched using more advanced name-comparison methods.

Knowledge Graph

What is this?

This is an interactive map that shows how the 30 ferrofluid topics connect to each other. It's like a web where related topics are linked together.

How to read it

  • Each box is one topic. Bigger boxes mean more documents are in that topic.
  • Colors show which broad category the topic belongs to: green = Materials, blue = Computer Modeling, orange = Experiments, pink = Applications, purple = Reviews & Books.
  • Red boxes are the gaps — topics that have research papers but NO patents. These could be opportunities for new inventions.
  • Lines between boxes mean some documents belong to both topics. Thicker lines = more documents in common.

You can click and drag the boxes around, scroll to zoom in or out, and hover over any box to see more details about that topic.
